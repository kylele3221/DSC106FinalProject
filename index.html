What is a monsoon?

Monsoons are seasonal wind and rainfall patterns that bring life-sustaining rains to large parts of the world.

Indian Summer Monsoon (ISM)

Each summer, moist winds rush inland from the Indian Ocean, bringing intense rains to South Asia between about 5°N and 35°N, from 60°E to 100°E.

West African Monsoon (WAM)

Over West Africa, the monsoon delivers rainfall to the Sahel, from roughly 5°N to 20°N and 20°W to 20°E, sustaining crops and river systems.

South American Monsoon (SAMS)

In South America, the monsoon system spans the Amazon and central Brazil, between about 5°N and 25°S, and 70°W to 35°W, shaping one of the world’s largest rainforest regions.

Well, Why Does this Even Matter?

According to NASA's Global Precipitation Measurement, rising temperatures intensify the Earth's water cycle. Increased evaporation fuels more frequent and intense storms in some regions, while drying others—meaning wet areas get wetter, and dry areas get drier. These shifts significantly impact agriculture, flooding, and global water security.

1. Agriculture: Increasingly Unpredictable and Vulnerable

In Wet Regions

  • Flooded fields: Excess water drowns crops and damages roots
  • Soil erosion: Heavy rainfall strips away fertile topsoil
  • Plant diseases: Warm, humid conditions promote fungal outbreaks
  • Crop yield instability: Farmers face more frequent losses from storm damage

In Dry Regions

  • Drought stress: Insufficient water reduces crop yields
  • Reduced soil moisture: Dry, compacted soil becomes less productive
  • Shifting crop zones: Some crops can no longer grow in former regions
  • Higher irrigation demand: Farmers forced to rely more on groundwater or rivers that may also be shrinking

2. Flooding: More Extreme and More Common Where Rain Increases

In Wet Areas

  • Intense rainstorms: Intense rainstorms dump large volumes of water quickly
  • River overflow: River basins flood more often
  • Urban flooding: Stormwater systems become overwhelmed
  • Coastal compound flooding: Heavy rain combines with rising sea-levels

In Dry Regions

  • Flash flooding: Hard, dry soils absorb water poorly, causing sudden floods during rare storms
  • Overall trend: Chronic flooding decreases, while drought becomes dominant

3. Water Security: Too Much Water in Some Places, Too Little in Others

In Wet Regions

  • Inconsistent water supply: Water supply becomes inconsistent (long dry spells punctuated by huge storms)
  • Contamination risks: Floods contaminate drinking water with sewage or pollutants
  • Infrastructure stress: Reservoirs and dams face overflow risks

In Dry Regions

  • Chronic shortages: ess rainfall and higher evaporation dry out rivers, lakes, and aquifers
  • Groundwater depletion: People resort to pumping more groundwater than nature can replenish
  • Increased competition: Agriculture, cities, and ecosystems vie for limited water
  • Migration pressures: Water scarcity may force communities to relocate
